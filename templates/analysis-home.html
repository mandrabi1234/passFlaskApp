Analytics Explained

    In layman’s terms, xG consists of shot attempts that have been weighted for shot quality. It employs various characteristics of shots being taken together with historical data said shot-types to predict the likelihood of a given shot resulting in a goal. As aforementioned, xG is basically an averaged probability of a shot resulting in a goal, providing a more accurate and objective method of predicting match outcomes than by simply using individual stats. xG is most often employed to assess various scenarios such as:
    1. Predicting upcoming match outcomes via historical data pertaining to the competing teams.
    2. Assessing a given player or team’s “true” performance, regardless of short-term results or individual on-pitch occurrences. This may be viewed through a seasonal or match-based lens.
    3. Assessing how effectively a team’s defense prevents opposition sides from converting goal-scoring chances, as a means of better understanding their overall defensive quality.
    4. Assessing which players are more effective than their shot quality would suggest. Such is frequently employed to distinguish consistent goal-scoring prowess from a flash in the pan, as it were...
    Elaborating further: xG provides an objective look at how many goals a player should be scoring, based on their shot quality. Player ability/performance may be assessed by comparing expected and actual output. More specifically, if a player:
    • Underperforms xG (scores fewer goals than expected), one may assume that there's more to come from them.
    • Overperforms xG (scores more goals than expected), one may assume that their output isn't actually indicative of their true goal-scoring ability.
    • Performs on par with xG (scores about as many goals as expected), one may assume that their current output is on par with their true ability.
    Take, for example, two players with the same average expected goals per game—e.g. 2.5 xGp90. However, one is performing on par with expectation, while the other is grossly overperforming. Upon assessing the two, the former bears greater consideration, as their performance against expectation reflects a very strong true goal-scoring ability. On the other hand, the latter is running a significant run of form and will almost assuredly fizzle out. For context, overperforming 2.5 xGp90 means they're almost averaging a hat trick per match (better than Messi or Ronaldo).

The selected ML model is a Random Forest regressor, trained and tested, respectively, on a publicly available European soccer data set6 and an Oxy-specific data set built from scratch. The latter was constructed by manually scraping the team’s 2019-2020 season’s game footage, courtesy of Occidental College Men’s Soccer Coach, Rod LaFaurie. Before going any further, it’s important to understand the specifics of a Random Forest model.

Broadly speaking, it is an ensemble ML algorithm which performs classification or regression tasks by using multiple decision trees—hence, Random Forest—as well as a technique known as Bootstrap and Aggregation, or bagging. Furthermore, it’s a meta-estimator, meaning the final output is a result of combining multiple prediction outputs. The concept behind this is to improve the final output by using multiple decision trees as base learning models and combining their results, rather than just using any individual tree. In this particular case—i.e. regression—the final output is the mean prediction of the individual trees. Random Forests present a variety of advantages, many of which are applicable to this specific project, including:

  • Producing highly accurate results.
  • Running efficiently on large databases (especially helpful given the size of the training set)
  • Handling very large amounts of input variables without deletion (useful with an expanding project scope).
  • Estimating what variables are important for classification output.
  • Limiting the number of features that can be split at each node to some percentage of the total, thereby avoiding over-reliance on any individual feature and ensuring fair use of all potentially predictive features.
  • Having each tree draw a random sample from the original data set when generating splits, thereby introducing further randomness that prevents overfitting.
